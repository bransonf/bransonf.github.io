History Final Guide

New Imperialism

The colonial expansion of European powers, the United States and Japan in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. The period featured an unprecedented pursuit of overseas territorial acquisitions.

The Opium Wars

two wars faught between western powers and China after China tried to restrict imports (especially opium). China lost both times and forced to make major concessions.

Berlin Conference

a meeting between European nations to create rules on how to peacefully divide Africa among them for colonization. The conference was convened by Portugal but led by Otto von Bismarck, chancellor of the newly united Germany.

Emperor Menelik II

Ethiopian emperor who preserved the independence of his people by defeating a major Italian military expedition and who strengthened his kingdom through expansion and political and economic modernization.

Otto Von Bismarck

ruled first Prussia and then all of Germany. He was a master strategist and initiated divisive wars with Denmark, Austria and France to unite 39 independent German states under Prussian leadership. Although an arch-conservative, he introduced progressive reforms such as universal male suffrage and establishing the first welfare state. He manipulated European rivals to make Germany a world power, but in doing so laid the groundwork for both World Wars.

Triple Alliance

Germany, Italy and the Austro-Hungarian Empire

Triple Entente

Russia, France and Britain

Schlieffen Plan

The name was given to German wars plans influenced by Field Marshall Alfred von Schlieffen and his thinking on an invasion of France and Blegium. He was chief of the general staff of the Germany army from 1891 to 1906

Archduke Ferdinand

was the heir to the throne of Austria-Hungary. He was assinated by a Serbian nationalist, which causes Austria-Hungary to declare war on Siberia, which leds to eaches allies declaring war on each other

Total War

the idea that the enemy can be best defeated by attacking everything, including soldiers, citizens, food, supplies and more

Tsar Nicholas II

last tsar of Russia under Romanov rule. His poor handling of Bloody Sunday and Russia's role in World War I led to his abdication and execution

Vladmir I. Lenin

a Russian lawyer, revolutionary, leader of the Bolshevik party and of the October Revolution. He was the first leader of the USSR and the government that took over Russia in 1917. His ideas became known as Leninism

Treaty of Brest-Litovsk

a treaty signed between the new Bolshevik government of Soviet Russia and the Central Powers (Germany, Austria-Hungary, Bulgaria and the Ottoman Empire) which ended Russia's participation in World War I

Woodrow Wilson

the 28th president of the United States, he lead America through World War I and crafted the Versailles Treaty's Fourteen Point, the last of which created the League of Nations to ensure world peace. He also created the Federal Reserve and supported the 19th amendment, allowing women to vote.

Versailles Settlement

The 1919 treaty officially ended World War I. The immense penalties it placed on Germany are regarded as one of the causes of World War II

The Influenza Pandemic

was an outbreak of the H1N1 virus in 1918-1919 that killed many people.

League of Nations

an international organization founded after the Paris Peace Conference. The League's goals included disarmament, preventing war through collective security, settling disputes between countries through negotiation, diplomacy and improving global welfare

The Great Depression

the worst economic downturn in the history of the industrialized world, lasting from 1929 to 1939. It began with a stock market crash in October which sent Wall Street into a panic and wiped out millions of investors.

Franklin Roosevelt

was the 32nd president of the United States and served during the Great Depression, implementing his New Deal domestic agenda. He was also in office during WWII which ended shortly after he died in office

The New Deal

was a series of programs, public work projects, financial reforms and regualtions enacted by President Franklin D Roosevelt in the United States between 1933 and 1939. It helped relief, reform and recover from the great depression.

War Communism

refers to the policies, particularly economic, pursued by the Bolsheviks during the Civil War in response to the ideological and pragmatic demands of consolidating power.

The New Economic Policy

was the economic policy of the Soviet Union from 1921 to 1928, representing a temporary retreat from its previous policy of extreme centralization and doctrinaire socialism. Money was reintroduced, as it had been abolished under War Communism.

Benito Mussolini

Italian politician and journalist who was the leader of the National Fascist party. He ruled Italy as prime minister from 1922 to 1943. He constitutionally led the country undtil 1925 until he dropped the pretense of democracy and established a dictatorship.

Adolf Hitler

was a German politician and leader of the Nazi Party. He rose to power as Chancellor of Germany in 1933 and as Fuhrer in 1934. During his dictatorship, he initiated World War II by invading Poland. He was closely involved in military operations and was central to the Holocaust.

Mein Kampf

was an autobiographical manifesto by Nazi Party leader Adolf Hitler. The work describes the process by which Hitler becomes antisemitic and outlines his political ideology and future plans.

Enabling Act

was an amendment passed to the Weimar Constitution that gave the German cabinet (in effect, Hitler) the power to enact laws without the involvement of the lower house. It helped create Hitler's position as a dictator.

Nuremberg Laws

were antisemitic and racist laws in Nazi Germany, enacted by Hitler. It stripped Jews of citizenship in Germany.

Munich Conference

held in Munich, leaders from Great Britain, France and Italy agreed to allow Germany to annex certain areas of Czechoslovakia. It was the result of a long series of negotiations.

Neville Chamberlain

was a conservative statesman who served as Prime Minister of the United Kingdom. He was known for his foreign policy of appeasement and for signing the Munich agreement

Appeasement policy

diplomatic policy of making poltical or material concessions to an aggressive power in order to avoid conflict

Nazi-Soviet Pact

was a neutrality pact between Nazi Germany and the Soviet Union, signed in Moscow in 1939.

The Secret Protocol

defined the borders of the Soviet Union and German spheres of influecne accross Poland, Lithuania, Latvia, Estonia and Finland

Marshall Petain

was a French general officer who attained the postion of Marshal of France at the end of World War I. In collaboration with Nazi Germany, he served as the Chief of State of Vichy France from 1940 to 1944

Vichy France

the French state after Germany defeated and occupied it at the beginning of World War II. The Vichy was essentially a puppet of the Germans

Charles de Gaulle

was a French army officer and statesman who lead the French Resistance against Nazi Germany in World War II and chaired the Provisional Government of the French Republic from 1944 to 1946 to reestablish democracy in France.

Blitzkrieg

an intense military campaign intended to bring about swift victory

Final Solution

the Nazi policy of exterminating European Jews. Introduced by Heinrich Himmler and administed by Adolf Eichmann, the policy resulted in the genocide of 6 million Jews in concentration camps

Molotov

was a stalinist Soviet politician and diplomat, an Old Bolshevik and a leading figure in the Soviet government from the 1920s, when he rose to power as the protege of Joseph Stalin. He was the principal Soviet signatory of the Nazi-Soviet non-aggression pact of 1939.

Battle of Stalingrad

was the largest confrontation of World War II, in which Germany and its allies fought the Soviet Union for control of the city of Stalingrad. The Germans were flanked and ultimately surrendered.

Lend-Lease Act

allowed the US government to lend or lease (rather than sell) war supplies to any nation deemed vital to the defense of the United States.

Winston Churchill

was a British poltician, army officer and writer. He was the prime minister of the United Kingdom. He led Britain to victory in World War II.

Pearl Harbor

was an American naval base on Hawaii that was struck by Imperial Japanese Navy Air Service on December 7th, 1941, leading the United States to become involved in World War II

Teheran Conference

was a strategic meeting of Joseph Stalin, Franklin Roosevelt and Winston Churchill after the Anglo-Soviet Invastion of Iran.

Normandy Invasion

was the attack of Western Allies against Germany at the northern coast of France (Normandy). The allies were able to establish themselves along the coast in the first day of fighting, known as D-Day.

Yalta Conference

meeting between the United States, United Kingdom and Soviet Union to discuss the postwar reorganization of Germany and Europe.

Potsdam Conference

meeting again among the Allied Leaders following the surrender of Germany. It was to discuss punishing Germany and reseperating territories.

United Nations

established after World War II with the aim of preventing future wars, succeeding the ineffective League of Nations. It is an international peace keeping organization, also working in protecting human rights and delivering humanitarian aid.

Cold War

state of political hostility between Soviet bloc countries and US-led western powers from 1945 to 1990

Truman Doctrine

American foreign policy whose stated purpose was to counter Soviet geopolitical expansion during the Cold War.

Marshall Plan

was an American intiative passed in 1948 to aid Western Europe, in which the United Sates gave over 12 billion in economic assistance to help rebuild western European economies at the end of World War II.

NATO

North Atlantic Treaty Organization, the purpose of NATO is to create an intergovernmental milirary alliance between 29 North American and European Countries. It is a system of collective defence in which members pledge to defend others in the case of attack

Warsaw Pact

was a treaty establishing a mutual-defense organization composed originally of the Soviet Union, Albania, Bulgaria, Czechoslovakia, East Germany Hungary, Poland and Romania.

Vietnam War

The United States and other anti-communist allies supported south Vietnam is resisting the establishment of communism by north vietnam, who was backed by China and the Soviet Union.

Korean War

was a war between North and South Korea. The north was communist and supported by the Soviet Union and Chine. The south was capitalist and supported by the United States. They contested ownership of all of Korea and the North invaded the South. The war never officially ended, but a demilitarized zone was established and the north and south remain seperated.

Cuban Missle Crisis

was a confrontation between the United States and the Soviet Union initiated by the discovery of Soviet ballistic missiles in Cuba. This was the closest the world has ever come to full-scale nuclear warfare.

Mikhail Gorbachev

was a Russian and former Soviet politican. The eight and last leader of the Soviet Union. He is recognized as ending tensions between the US and Soviet Union and helping bring the Cold War to an end.

Glasnost

policy of more open consultative government and wider dissemination of information, initiated by leader Mikhail Gorbachev.

Perestroika

the practice of restructuring the economic and political system. Reffered to increased automation and labor efficiency but also lead to greater understanding of the financial markets and ending of central planning

Boris Yeltsin

was a Soviet and Russian politician who served as the first president of the Russian Federation from 1991 to 1999. He is known for transforming Russia's state socialist economy into a capitalist market economy.

Vladmir Putin

current president of Russia

Mao Zedong

was a communist revolutionary who became the founding father of the People's Republic of China which he ruled as the chairman of the Communist Party of China.

Guomindang

Chinese nationalist party

Chiang Kai-Shek

prominent member of the Kuomintang was a lieutenant of Sun Yat-sen in the revolution to overthrow the Beiyang government and reunify China.

China's Cultural Revolution

sociopoltical movement of the People's Republic of China and an attempt to purge the culture of capitalist and traditional elements from Chinese society.

Deng Xiaoping

following the death of Mao Zedong, he gradually rose to power and led China through a series of far-reaching market-economy reforms.

Josip Tito

was a Yugoslav communist revolutionary. During WWII he was the leader of the Partisans, regarded as the most effective resistance movement in occupied Europe.

Decolonization

the undoing of colonialism (where a nation establishes its domination on overseas territories)

Mahatma Gandhi

indian lawyer and anti-colonial nationalist and political ethicist who employed nonviolent resistance to lead the successful campaign for India's independence from British Rule.

Abdul Ghaffar Khan

was a Pashtun independence activist who worked to end the rule of the British Raj in India. He was a political and spiritual leader known for his nonviolent opposition; a lifelong pacifist and devout Muslim.

Non-Aligned Nations

a forum of 120 developing world states that are not aligned with or against any major power bloc.

Gamal Abdel Nasser

was the second President of Egypt, and helped overthrow the monarchy. He also introduced far reaching land reforms.

Jawaharlal Nehru

was a nationalist and prominent leader of left-wing factions of the Indian national congress. He called for complete independence from the British Raj and instigated the Congress's shift to the left.

Apartheid

was a system of institutionalised racial segregation that existed in South and South West Africa from 1948 to the 1990s. Apartheid was characterised by an authoritarian political culture based on White Supremacy.

Afrikaners

a South African ethnic group descended from predominantly Dutch settlers. They dominated South Africa's politicals and agriculture up until 1994.

Nelson Mandela

was a South African anti-apartheid revolutionary, political leader and philanthropist who served as president of South Africa from 1994 to 1999. He was the country's first black head of state and elected in a fully representative democratic election.

Economic Globalization

increasing interdependence of world economies as a result of the growing scale of cross-border trade of commodities and services, flow of international capital and wide and rapid spread of technologies

Migration

movement from one area to another, often in seeking better living conditions or economic opportunity.

Feminism

advocacy of women's rights on the basis of equality of the sexes

Fundamentalism

a form of a religion (especially Islam or Protestant Christianity) that upholds belief in the strict, literal interpretation of scripture.

Environmentalism

concern about and action aimed at protecting the environment

​ The influenza pandemic was known as the Spanish flu because of wartime censorship. The pandemic lead to post war disillusionment, and some sources suggest that it gave the Allies an advantage since the flu affected Germany worst. Governments adopted techniques like qurantining infected persons and closing ports. They banned many public events as well. Many regular people stepped up to treat people within their communities. Doctors still did not have the modern understanding of infectious disease that we now have, so it was hard to deduce that an infectious virus was being spread and no treatments or cures existed at the time. Since, however, our surveilance of pandemics has improved significantly, as has our understanding of the spread of infectious diseases.
